Personal Security Audit

Document version: 1.0 | Last updated: January 2026

Weight: 25% of final grade

Due: End of Week 5 (online) / Week 10 (in-person)

Format: Written report with evidence, 1500-2500 words

Overview

You will audit your own digital security and privacy practices, identify risks, and implement real changes. This is not a hypothetical exercise—you will make actual improvements to your digital life and document what you did.

Why this matters: The evaluation skills you're learning in this course apply to your own technology use. This assignment asks you to turn that critical lens on yourself.

Requirements

Part 1: Audit (What You Have)

Examine your current digital footprint across these areas:

Area What to Examine
Accounts What accounts do you have? Which have the same password? Which have MFA enabled? Which have you forgotten about?
Passwords How do you manage passwords? Are they unique? Strong? Stored securely?
Privacy Settings What are your settings on major platforms (social media, Google, Apple)? What data are you sharing?
App Permissions What permissions have you granted to apps on your phone? Location? Contacts? Camera? Microphone?
Data Exposure What can someone find about you through search? What have you posted publicly?
AI Tools What AI tools have you given access to your data? What did you agree to?

You don't have to share sensitive details. You can say "I found 3 accounts using the same weak password" without revealing which accounts or what the password was.

Part 2: Risk Assessment

For each issue you discover, assess:

  • Likelihood — How probable is this risk actually being exploited?
  • Severity — If it happened, how bad would it be?
  • Priority — Given likelihood and severity, how urgently should you address this?

Be realistic. Not every vulnerability is an emergency. Distinguish between theoretical risks and practical ones.

Part 3: Actions Taken

Implement at least five meaningful changes to improve your security or privacy. Examples:

  • Set up a password manager and migrate passwords
  • Enable MFA on important accounts
  • Review and restrict app permissions
  • Adjust privacy settings on social media
  • Delete unused accounts
  • Review and revoke AI tool access to your data
  • Set up a separate email for signups
  • Review what data Google/Apple has collected about you

Document with evidence. Screenshots (with sensitive info redacted), before/after comparisons, or other proof that you actually made changes. "I enabled MFA" without evidence will receive reduced credit.

Part 4: Reflection

Conclude with:

  • What surprised you most about your digital footprint?
  • What trade-offs did you make (convenience vs. security)?
  • What will you do differently going forward?
  • What risks did you decide to accept, and why?

Document Structure

  1. Introduction — Brief overview of your approach
  2. Audit Findings — What you discovered across the six areas
  3. Risk Assessment — Prioritized list of issues with severity/likelihood analysis
  4. Actions Taken — What you changed, with evidence
  5. Reflection — What you learned, trade-offs made, future intentions

What Makes a Strong Audit

  • Thoroughness — You examined multiple areas, not just the obvious ones
  • Realistic risk assessment — Neither paranoid nor dismissive
  • Meaningful actions — Changes that actually improve security, with evidence
  • Honest reflection — Acknowledging trade-offs and accepted risks

What to Avoid

  • Superficial audit — Only checking one or two areas
  • No evidence — Claiming changes without proof
  • Unrealistic risk assessment — Treating everything as critical or nothing as important
  • Trivial changes — Five minor tweaks don't equal one meaningful improvement

Privacy Note

This assignment asks you to examine personal information. You control what you share in your submission:

  • Redact account names, passwords, or other sensitive details in screenshots
  • Describe findings in general terms where appropriate
  • Focus on the process and learning, not exposing your personal data

The goal is demonstrating that you conducted a thorough audit and made real improvements—not revealing your secrets.
