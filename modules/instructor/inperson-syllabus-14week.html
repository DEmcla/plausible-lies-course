Thinking in the Age of AI

14-Week Semester | 3 Credits

Document version: 1.0 | Last updated: January 2026

Course Overview

This course teaches you to critically evaluate AI and digital tools using a transferable framework. No coding required. You'll learn to ask the right questions about any technology—current or future.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course, you will be able to:

  1. Explain how different AI models work and select appropriate tools for specific tasks
  2. Apply a systematic evaluation framework to assess any technology's claims, limitations, and trade-offs
  3. Communicate findings through professional documents, structured reports, and clear presentations
  4. Interpret and critique data visualizations, including AI-generated graphs, for validity and misleading representations
  5. Design and conduct controlled comparisons of AI tools, documenting methodology and drawing evidence-based conclusions
  6. Evaluate source reliability across AI outputs, training data claims, and technical documentation

Class Format

Classes combine brief lectures, hands-on activities, and discussion. Come prepared to engage—this is not a course you can absorb passively.

Typical class structure:

  • First 15-20 min: Concepts and context
  • Middle 30-40 min: Hands-on activity or exercise
  • Final 15-20 min: Discussion and synthesis

Unit 1: Foundations Weeks 1-3

Big question: What is AI, really? How do different types of AI models work, and why does that matter for how we evaluate them?

Week Topics Activities & Readings
Week 1 What is AI? Separating hype from reality. Types of models: LLMs, image generators, classifiers, recommendation systems. Reading: IBM: What Are Large Language Models?
Activity: Model Matching Exercise
Setup: Portfolio document structure
Week 2 How training works conceptually: data in, patterns out, no understanding. What training data reveals about capabilities and biases. Reading: IBM: What Is AI Bias?
Activity: Training Data Detective
Week 3 Introduction to the evaluation framework: Function, Data, Failures, Access, Terms, Broader Impact. Reading: Evaluation Framework document
Activity: Framework walkthrough with familiar tool
Portfolio Checkpoint 1 (End of Week 3): Cover page, working table of contents, section headings for all six outcomes. Document structure in place.

Unit 2: Testing Claims Weeks 4-6

Big question: How do we test whether AI tools actually do what they claim? What counts as evidence?

Week Topics Activities & Readings
Week 4 Designing controlled comparisons: same prompt, multiple models. Why comparison matters. Reading: AIMultiple: Reproducible AI
Activity: Reproducibility Test (Claude vs ChatGPT vs Gemini)
Week 5 Documenting methodology: what you did, what you found, what it means. Reproducibility and why "it worked for me" isn't evidence. Reading: Calling Bullshit: selected modules
Activity: Prompt Sensitivity exercise
Week 6 Applying the full evaluation framework. Structured tool evaluation. Activity: Work on AI Tool Audit
In-class: Peer feedback on audit drafts
AI Tool Audit Due (End of Week 6): Full evaluation of an AI tool using the framework. 25% of grade.
Portfolio Checkpoint 2 (End of Week 6): At least two artifacts with reflections. Table of contents updates correctly.

Unit 3: Visualizations & Data Weeks 7-9

Big question: How can data visualizations mislead us—and how do we spot it?

Week Topics Activities & Readings
Week 7 How AI generates graphs and charts. Understanding the data-to-visualization relationship. Reading: ThoughtSpot: How to Identify Misleading Graphs
Activity: Excel Visualization Lesson (hands-on)
Week 8 Spotting BS: misleading axes, cherry-picked data, statistical insignificance. The "Make It Lie" challenge. Activity: Make It Lie (Excel)
Activity: Graph or Garbage (evaluate AI charts)
Week 9 Evaluating published benchmarks and performance claims. When numbers lie. Reading: Nature: Is your AI benchmark lying to you?
Activity: Benchmark BS Detection
Portfolio Checkpoint 3 (End of Week 9): At least four artifacts with reflections. Table of figures if applicable. Consistent formatting throughout.

Unit 4: Communication & Synthesis Weeks 10-12

Big question: How do we communicate technical findings clearly and effectively?

Week Topics Activities & Readings
Week 10 Professional documentation: structuring findings, writing for different audiences. Reading: Document360: Technical Writing for Non-Technical Audiences
Activity: Critique the Explanation
Week 11 Practical document skills: Word styles, accessibility, maintaining consistency. AI privacy practices and data sharing decisions. Reading: USC Libraries: Writing Methodology Sections
Activity: Security audit preparation
Week 12 Peer review and methodology documentation. Can someone reproduce your work? Activity: Methodology Audit (peer review)
In-class: Security audit sharing (opt-in)
AI Privacy & Security Audit Due (End of Week 10): Audit your AI tool usage and make informed privacy decisions. 25% of grade.

Unit 5: Application Weeks 13-14

Big question: Can you apply everything you've learned to a completely new tool?

Week Topics Activities & Readings
Week 13 Framework review and preparation. Synthesis: connecting all course concepts. Activity: Review AI Tool Audit feedback
Activity: Portfolio synthesis reflection (start)
Week 14 Tool Adaptability Exercise. Novel tool revealed Monday; evaluation due end of week. Assessment: Tool Adaptability Exercise
Final: Complete portfolio
Tool Adaptability Exercise (Week 14): Evaluate a novel AI tool under time pressure. 30% of grade. Tool revealed Monday of Week 14.
Portfolio Checkpoint 4 - Final (End of Week 14): All six artifacts, all reflections, synthesis reflection. Complete, polished document. 20% of grade.

Weekly activities: 10% penalty per day late, up to 3 days. After 3 days, no credit.

Major assessments: 10% penalty per day late, up to 5 days. After 5 days, no credit.

Portfolio checkpoints: Must be submitted to receive feedback. Late checkpoints receive reduced feedback.

Academic Integrity

You will use AI tools extensively in this course—that's the point. However:

  • The evaluation, analysis, and conclusions must be your own thinking
  • You may use AI to help with writing mechanics, not to write your analysis
  • Always disclose how you used AI assistance in your submissions
  • Passing off AI-generated analysis as your own is academic dishonesty
